In the reptile house, the crocodile holds the crowd — parents and children press close to the glass, enthralled. Just behind them, by the iguana enclosure, an elderly lady leans on her walker, taking in the quiet corner.

She lifts her hand, holding it beside the iguana's twisted claws. “Oh,” she says with a chuckle, “you've got rheumatoid arthritis too, I see.” A child darts over, sending the iguana scuttling away, claws clacking quickly on the rocks.

The lady laughs, surprised by the burst of energy in her unlikely companion, feeling a quiet, shared strength in their crooked hands and well-worn joints. With a smile, she steadies her grip and turns, comforted by the company.
